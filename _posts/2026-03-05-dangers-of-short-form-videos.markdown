A friend recently asked me to compile a list of academic studies that show the dangers of TikTok and other short-form video platforms. 

Unfortunately, many people are still oblivious to the acute (motivation, mental degeneration) and indirect (less socialization, gateway to other addictive behaviors, deterioration in life outcomes such as sleep scores) dangers to children, teenagers, and adults. Some people may even think that warnings about the dangers are ungrounded and overblown. So here we go, there is vast academic literature proving the dangers.

## Cognitive Impairments

### Sustained Attention Deficits

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis covering 71 studies and over 98,000 participants found a moderate-to-strong negative correlation between short-form video engagement and cognitive performance. The strongest negative associations were with attention and inhibitory control.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### Reduced Executive Control

An EEG study measuring brain activation during short-video application use found that the neurological components associated with short-video addiction directly and negatively predicted adolescents' cognitive control and executive functioning.

> Zhong, Y., et al. (2024). "Neural Correlates of Short-Video Application Addiction and Adolescents' Executive Functions." *Proceedings of the International Conference of the Learning Sciences (ICLS)*.

### Lower Working Memory

A cross-sectional study of over 1,000 youth confirmed that moderate to high levels of short-form video addiction significantly impaired memory function. Diminished attention was the primary mediating factor between the addiction and loss of working memory capacity.

> Al-Leimon, et al. (2025). "Reels to Remembrance: Attention Partially Mediates the Relationship Between Short-Form Video Addiction and Memory Function Among Youth." *Healthcare (Basel)*, 13(3), 252.

## Neurological Impact and the Dopamine Loop

Researchers are increasingly studying what is colloquially known as "TikTok Brain" -- how algorithmic content delivery alters neural pathways.

### Dopamine-Driven Addiction Loops and Altered Brain Connectivity

A scoping review on internet and short-video addiction revealed significant alterations in the Default Mode Network (DMN). Addicted users showed hyper-connectivity in regions like the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) at rest, and an inability to suppress this network during focused tasks. This indicates a fundamental decoupling of cognitive control from self-regulation.

> Smith, A., et al. (2026). "The neurobiology of internet addiction: a scoping review of developmental and gender-specific mechanisms." *Frontiers in Psychology*.

### Reduced Risk Aversion

Neuroimaging studies have demonstrated that individuals with symptoms of short-video addiction show decreased activity in the precuneus, a brain region involved in evaluating risks and self-reflection. This makes heavily addicted users less sensitive to negative consequences and biases them toward immediate gratification.

> Zhang, Y., & Li, X. (2025). "Decision-making and reward processing in short-video addiction." Cited in multiple reviews, including *Taylor & Francis* systematic reviews on cognitive functioning.

## Mental Health and Behavioral Harms

### Anxiety and Depression

The same large-scale 2025 meta-analysis (71 studies) concluded that frequent SFV use is consistently linked to measurable increases in symptoms of anxiety, stress, and depression. The fast-paced, emotionally charged nature of the content is highly associated with emotional dysregulation.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### Academic Burnout and "Lying Flat"

Psychological research has highlighted a direct correlation between the "TikTok Brain" phenomenon and academic exhaustion. Prolonged immersion in instant entertainment weakens the ability to delay gratification, fostering avoidance, passive coping behaviors, and a documented new type of learning burnout.

> Mao, Y., & Liao, X. (2026). "Academic burnout in the TikTok era: a battle between pleasure and concentration" & "Scrolling to apathy? The relationship between short video addiction and the 'lying flat' tendency among Chinese university students." *Frontiers in Psychology*.

### Sleep Disruption

Systematic reviews confirm that the high physiological arousal and blue light exposure associated with rapid video consumption displace sleep. This loss of sleep further degrades cognitive repair and emotional resilience, compounding both mental fog and depressive symptoms -- especially in developing brains.

> Arouch, S., et al. (2025). "The Impact of Short-Form Video Use on Cognitive and Mental Health Outcomes: A Systematic Review." *medRxiv* (Preprint/Review).

## The Bottom Line

The evidence is converging: excessive short-form video consumption erodes attention, weakens memory, rewires reward circuitry, and fuels anxiety and depression. The algorithmic feed is designed to maximize engagement, not well-being. Being aware of these effects is the first step toward a healthier relationship with these platforms.
