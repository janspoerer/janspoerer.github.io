A friend recently asked me to compile a list of academic studies that show the dangers of TikTok and other short-form video platforms. 

Unfortunately, many people are still oblivious to the acute (motivation, mental degeneration) and indirect (less socialization, gateway to other addictive behaviors, deterioration in life outcomes) dangers to children, teenagers, and adults. Some people may even think that warnings about the dangers are ungrounded and overblown. So here we go, there is vast academic literature proving the dangers.

## Cognitive Impairments

### Sustained Attention Deficits

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis covering 71 studies and over 98,000 participants found a moderate negative correlation between short-form video engagement and cognitive performance. The strongest negative associations were with attention and inhibitory control.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### Reduced Working Memory

An EEG study measuring brain activation during short-video application use found that the neurological components associated with short-video addiction directly and negatively predicted adolescents' working memory. The researchers noted that, interestingly, problematic short-video application use did not show a significant negative relationship with cognitive control.

> Zhong, Y., et al. (2024). "Neural Correlates of Short-Video Application Addiction and Adolescents' Executive Functions." *Proceedings of the International Conference of the Learning Sciences (ICLS)*.

### Attention as a Mediator

A cross-sectional study of over 1,000 youth confirmed that moderate to high levels of short-form video addiction significantly impaired memory function. Diminished attention was found to be a partial mediating factor between the addiction and loss of memory capacity.

> Al-Leimon, et al. (2025). "Reels to Remembrance: Attention Partially Mediates the Relationship Between Short-Form Video Addiction and Memory Function Among Youth." *Healthcare (Basel)*, 13(3), 252.

## Neurological Impact and the Dopamine Loop

Researchers are increasingly studying what is colloquially known as "TikTok Brain" -- how algorithmic content delivery alters neural pathways.

### Dopamine-Driven Addiction Loops and Altered Brain Connectivity

A scoping review on internet addiction broadly revealed significant alterations in the Default Mode Network (DMN). Addicted users showed hyper-connectivity in regions like the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) at rest, and an inability to suppress this network during focused tasks. This indicates a fundamental decoupling of cognitive control from self-regulation, mechanisms which researchers frequently extrapolate to specific behaviors like short-video addiction.

> Jin, W., Chai, W., Wu, J., Chen, H., & Ding, M. (2026). "The neurobiology of internet addiction: a scoping review of developmental and gender-specific mechanisms." *Frontiers in Psychology*.

## Mental Health and Behavioral Harms

### Anxiety and Depression

The same large-scale 2025 meta-analysis (71 studies) concluded that frequent SFV use is linked to measurable increases in symptoms of anxiety, stress, and depression. The fast-paced, emotionally charged nature of the content is associated with emotional dysregulation. However, the review noted an important caveat: it is highly plausible that individuals with pre-existing anxiety or depression may turn to short-form videos as a coping mechanism, meaning strict causality is not fully established.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### The "Lying Flat" Tendency

Psychological research has highlighted a direct correlation between short-video addiction and the "lying flat" phenomenon—a social trend characterized by a rejection of overwork and a preference for a detached, apathetic lifestyle. Prolonged immersion provides immediate gratification that can replace long-term goals, acting as a psychological refuge that fosters lifestyle disengagement and apathy.

> Xu, J., et al. (2026). "Scrolling to apathy? The relationship between short video addiction and the 'lying flat' tendency among Chinese university students." *Frontiers in Psychology*.

## The Bottom Line

The evidence is converging: excessive short-form video consumption erodes attention, weakens working memory, alters reward circuitry, and fuels anxiety and apathy. The algorithmic feed is designed to maximize engagement, not well-being.
