A friend recently asked me to compile a list of academic studies that show the potential harms of TikTok and other short-form video (SFV) platforms. 

Unfortunately, many people are still oblivious to the potential harms and indirect risks (such as less socialization, gateway to other addictive behaviors, deterioration in life outcomes) to children, teenagers, and adults. Some people may even think that warnings about the potential harms are ungrounded and overblown. So here we go, there is a growing body of evidence highlighting these risk markers. Note that most studies in this area are cross-sectional or correlational, so they do not prove causation.

## Cognitive Impairments

### Sustained Attention Deficits

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis covering 71 studies and over 98,000 participants found a moderate negative correlation between SFV engagement and cognitive performance. The strongest negative associations were with attention and inhibitory control.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### Reduced Working Memory

An EEG study measuring brain activation during SFV application use found that the neurological components associated with SFV addiction were negatively associated with adolescents' working memory. The researchers noted that, interestingly, problematic SFV application use did not show a significant negative relationship with cognitive control.

> Xu, Z., Liu, X., Chen, J., & Zhang, Y. (2024). "Neural Correlates of Short-Video Application Addiction and Adolescents' Executive Functions." *Proceedings of the International Conference of the Learning Sciences (ICLS)*. [[ISLS Repository](https://repository.isls.org//handle/1/11096)]

### Attention as a Mediator

A cross-sectional study of over 1,000 university students found that higher SFV addiction scores were associated with poorer memory function. Diminished attention partially mediated the relationship between the addiction and loss of memory capacity.

> Al-Leimon, et al. (2025). "Reels to Remembrance: Attention Partially Mediates the Relationship Between Short-Form Video Addiction and Memory Function Among Youth." *Healthcare (Basel)*, 13(3), 252. [[doi:10.3390/healthcare13030252](https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare13030252)]

## Neurological Impact and the Dopamine Loop

Researchers are increasingly studying what is colloquially known as "TikTok Brain" -- how algorithmic content delivery alters neural pathways.

### Dopamine-Driven Addiction Loops and Altered Brain Connectivity

A scoping review on internet addiction broadly revealed significant alterations in the Default Mode Network (DMN). Addicted users showed hyper-connectivity in regions like the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) at rest, and an inability to suppress this network during focused tasks. This pattern has been interpreted as consistent with altered self-regulation or difficulty disengaging the DMN during tasks. These findings are not specific to SFV use; applying them to SFV is an inference.

> Jin, W., Chai, W., Wu, J., Chen, H., & Ding, M. (2026). "The neurobiology of internet addiction: a scoping review of developmental and gender-specific mechanisms." *Frontiers in Psychology*. [[doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1729470](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1729470)]

## Mental Health and Behavioral Harms

### Anxiety and Depression

The same large-scale 2025 meta-analysis (71 studies) concluded that frequent SFV use is linked to measurable increases in symptoms of anxiety, stress, and depression. The fast-paced, emotionally charged nature of the content is associated with emotional dysregulation. However, the review noted an important caveat: it is highly plausible that individuals with pre-existing anxiety or depression may turn to SFVs as a coping mechanism, meaning strict causality is not fully established.

> Nguyen, L., et al. (2025). "Feeds, feelings, and focus: A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the cognitive and mental health correlates of short-form video use." *Psychological Bulletin*, 151(9), 1125-1146.

### The "Lying Flat" Tendency

Psychological research has highlighted a direct correlation between SFV addiction and the "lying flat" phenomenon—a social trend characterized by a rejection of overwork and a preference for a detached, apathetic lifestyle. Prolonged immersion provides immediate gratification that can replace long-term goals, acting as a psychological refuge that fosters lifestyle disengagement and apathy.

> Xu, J., et al. (2026). "Scrolling to apathy? The relationship between short video addiction and the 'lying flat' tendency among Chinese university students." *Frontiers in Psychology*. [[doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1685559](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1685559)]

## The Bottom Line

Evidence increasingly suggests that heavy SFV use is associated with poorer attention/working memory and worse mental-health symptoms; causality and mechanisms are still being studied. The algorithmic feed is designed to maximize engagement, not well-being.
